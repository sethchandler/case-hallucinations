Practical Guide to Using the Case Hallucinations App

This document outlines the capabilities and limitations of our case hallucinations detection app. This tool is designed to serve as an initial screening mechanism to help identify "hallucinated" or non-existent case citations.

Critical Limitation: This app makes NO effort to determine whether cited cases actually support or are relevant to what you are asserting in your text. A case citation can be completely "accurate" but you can be citing the case for a proposition that the actual case does not support.
Important: It is essential to understand that the application is both underinclusive and overinclusive. It is underinclusive because it will not find every possible citation error. It is overinclusive because it may flag citations that are technically correct but formatted in a way that differs from its database. It is a valuable tool, but not a substitute for final human review.

How the Application Flags Potential Errors

The application functions by performing a very strict, literal comparison of the citations in your document against its database. Its primary value is in identifying case citations that do not exist.

However, its precision means it is overinclusive in flagging issues. If a case name in your document differs from the database entry by even a single comma, a period, or a minor abbreviation (e.g., "Corp." vs. "Corporation"), the application will flag it as a potential mismatch.

What this means for you: A flagged citation is not necessarily a hallucination or an error. It is an alert that requires your manual review. The flag may indicate a substantive error, or it may simply be a minor stylistic difference.

What the Application Does Not Check

The application is underinclusive and is limited to verifying standard, complete case law citations. It will not find errors or hallucinations for the following types of citations, as it simply ignores them:

  • Statutes, regulations, or rules of procedure.
  • Law review articles, treatises, or other secondary sources.
  • Short-form citations such as Id. or Supra.
  • Incomplete case citations where a volume or page number is missing (e.g., 22 U.S. ___).
What this means for you: You must continue to verify these types of citations manually. The application offers no opinion on their validity.

Limits on Document Size and Processing Speed

To ensure the application remains available and responsive for all users, there are practical limits on how much content you can analyze at one time.

1. Limit on Citations Per Document. The application will check a maximum of 250 citations in any single document you submit for analysis.
  • What will work: Submitting a brief with 200 citations. All 200 will be checked.
  • What will not work: Submitting a brief with 300 citations. The application will check the first 250 citations it finds and will not check the remaining 50.
  • Workaround: For documents with more than 250 citations, split them into smaller sections and analyze each section separately.
2. Limit on Document Length. The application can only process a document up to approximately 64,000 characters, which is roughly 40-50 pages of standard legal text.
  • What will work: Submitting a 30-page motion for analysis.
  • What will not work: Submitting a 75-page appellate brief. The system will reject the document entirely.
  • Workaround: For any document longer than 50 pages, you must split it into smaller sections for analysis.
3. Limit on Processing Rate. The system manages a pace of checking approximately 60 citations per minute across all your submissions.
  • What will work: Submitting several documents for analysis one after the other, with a brief pause in between.
  • What will not work: Attempting to submit a dozen documents simultaneously or in very rapid succession. If you exceed the processing-rate limit, the application will temporarily prevent you from starting a new analysis. It will usually display a message indicating when you can try again.
  • Workaround: If you have many documents to check, submit them sequentially. Wait for one analysis to complete before starting the next.

This tool is provided for educational and research purposes. Always consult qualified legal professionals for legal matters.
